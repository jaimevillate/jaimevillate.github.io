You might probably be familiar with the idea that a list of 3 real numbers (x, y, z) is a vector in space. If that is the case, I ask you to forget about that while you read this chapter, where vectors are introduced as geometrical objects independent of any system of coordinates. Using the geometrical properties of vectors, without reference to any system of coordinates, will let you gain more insight into their physical meaning. After studying vectors from a geometrical point of view, we will introduce coordinate systems in the next chapter and we will make extensive use of vector components in later chapters.

1.1. Vectors and scalars

Physical quantities whose magnitude is given by a real value are called scalars. Examples of scalar quantities are time, temperature and pressure. Each scalar has units and if the units of two different scalars are compatible, they can be combined with the same rules as for real numbers: for example, we can add a mass, in kg, with other masses also in kg; we can divide a distance in km by a time in hours, to give a speed in km/h, and so on.

In contrast to scalars, a vector is a physical quantity that in addition to its magnitude it also has a direction. The magnitude of the vector is sometimes also called norm or module. A typical example of a vector is a force; the magnitude of a force is measured in units of force, i. e. newton (N) in the international system of units. The direction of the force is the direction in which it is exerted. We will denote vectors by letters with an arrow over them ,for instance a force F→, and their magnitude by enclosing the symbol inside vertical bars: |F→|. In cases when F cannot be confused with other scalar quantities, we will simply write the magnitude of F→ as F. The magnitude of a vector is a scalar.

Figure 1.1 shows three forces; the weight W→ of a 10 kg sphere, which has magnitude of 98 N and points downwards in the vertical, and the forces F→1 and F→2 exerted by one the feet of a woman and a man on the ground.

Two vectors are considered equal if their magnitude and direction are the same. In the example in figure 1.1, the two forces F→1 and F→2 might be equal if they have the same magnitude and direction.

1.2 Addition of vectors

The sum of two vectors is defined as another vector which can be obtained by placing one of the vectors after the other and then joining the initial point of the first vector with the final point of the second one. The right-hand side of figure 1.2 shows the sum A→+B→ of two vectors A→ and B→, which was obtained by displacing B→ until its initial point is at the final point of A→.

In the case of forces, the vector sum of two forces gives a force that produces the same effect as the two forces combined.

The addition of vectors has the following four important properties:

Commutative law: A→+B→=B→+A→
Associative law: A→+(B→+C→)=(A→+B→)+C→
Existence of a vector zero:  A→+0→=A→
Existence of negatives: A→+(−A→)=0→

The commutative law can be shown by completing a parallelogram with the two vectors A→ and B→ as shown in figure 1.3. The sum of the vectors is the diagonal of the parallelogram, which is obtained by either placing B→ after A→ or by placing A→ after B→.

The proof of the associative law is shown in figure 1.4, which shows three vectors A→, B→ and C→, together with the sums A→+B→ and B→+C→. The sum of the three vectors can then be simply written A→+B→+C→ without any need for parenthesis.

The vector zero, 0→ is the only vector than when added to any vector A→ will not change that vector. It is unique and it has magnitude equal to zero; it is also the only vector that has no direction. For any vector A→ there is always a unique vector, called its negative, such that when added to A→ the result will be the vector zero. The negative of A→ is denoted −A→ and it is easy to see that it must be a vector with the same magnitude |A→|, but with opposite direction to A→ (see figure 1.5).

In the case of forces, the uniqueness of the negative means that the only way to cancel the effect of a force is by adding another force with the same magnitude but opposite direction.

The subtraction of two vectors can be defined as the sum of the first vector and the negative of the second one:

A→−B→=A→+(−B→)
(1.1)

Thus, A→−B→ can be obtained by placing −B→ at the final point of A→, as in the left-hand side of figure 1.6. The right-hand side of figure 1.6 shows that A→−B→ can also be obtained as one of the diagonals of the parallelogram obtained with A→ and B→. As we saw before, the diagonal that begins from the common point of the two vectors is A→+B→; and the diagonal that goes from the end point of B→ to the end point of A→ is A→−B→.

Scalar multiplication

When we add a vector A→ with itself n times, the result is a vector in the same direction of A→ but with a magnitude n times bigger and we write the result as nA→. If n is a negative integer, we interpret na→ as summing |n| times the negative of the vector, −A→, thus leading to a vector in the opposite direction of A→ and with magnitude |n| times bigger than the magnitude of A→.

The generalization to any scalar k is that the product kA→ gives a vector with magnitude |k|A, in the same direction of A→, when k is positive, or in the opposite direction when k is negative. When k=0, the product kA→ gives the vector zero, 0→.

The following table shows the important properties of the product of scalars with vectors.

Unit multiplication: 1A→=A→
Associative law: k(pA→)=(kp)A→
Distributive law (vector addition):  k(A→+B→)=kA→+kB→
Distributive law (scalar addition): (k+p)A→=kA→+pA→

Dot product

The dot product between two vectors A→ and B→, denoted by A→·B→, gives a real number (scalar) equal to the product of the magnitudes of the two vectors, times the cosine of the angle between the directions of the two vectors:

A→·B→=ABcosθ
(1.2)

Figure 1.7 shows two vectors A→ and B→ and the angle θ between their directions. The projection of A→ in the direction of B→ is equal to Acosθ; notice that it can be negative when θ>π/2, namely when that projection points in the opposite direction of B→. The projection of B→ in the direction of A→ is Bcosθ. Therefore, the dot product A→·B→ is also equal to the projection of one of the vectors along the direction of the other, times the magnitude of that second vector. The dot product is also referred to as scalar product.

It will be positive if θ is an acute angle (less than π/2), negative if that angle is obtuse (greater than π/2) and zero when the two vectors are perpendicular (see figure 1.8).

If two vectors A→ and B→ have the same direction, then A→·B→=AB, while if they have opposite directions then A→·B→=−AB.

The dot product has the following properties:

Possible values: −AB≤A→·B→≤AB
Commutative law: A→·B→=B→·A→
Distribute law: A→·(B→+C→)=A→·B→+A→·C→
Scalar associativity:  k(A→·B→)=(kA→)·B→=A→·(kB→)
Positiveness: A→·A→=A2
