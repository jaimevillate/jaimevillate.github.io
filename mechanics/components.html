2.1 Unit vectors

A unit vector is a vector with magnitude equal to 1. Thus, what distinguishes different unit vectors is their direction and any possible direction in space can be associated to a unit vector. If we divide a vector A→ by its magnitude, the result is a unit vector, in the direction of A→, which we will dubbed as A^, with a circumflex accent rather than an arrow, to distinguish it from A→:

A^=A→A
(2.1)

Vector A→ is then characterized by a positive scalar A, its magnitude, and a unit vector A^ which defines its direction (see figure 2.1). The vector is the product of its magnitude and its unit vector: A→=AA^.

2.2 Vectors on a plane

If we choose a particular direction on the plane and call the unit vector in that direction ı^, any other unit vectors A^, B^, etc. can be defined with just one number, the angle between those unit vectors and ı^. In figure 2.2, the unit vector A^ makes and angle α with vector ı^, in the counter clock-wise direction. Rotating an angle α from the direction of ı^ in the clock-wise direction leads to another direction different from A^; to distinguish those two directions, we give a positive sign to one of them and a negative sign to the other. The unit vector ȷ^, perpendicular to ı^ defines the direction in which we consider the angle with ı^ positive. The angle β of unit vector B^ in figure 2.2 is then negative.

The directions of the two unit vectors ı^ and ȷ^ are usually called the x and y directions. Notice that every direction in space can be defined by a positive or a negative angle. In figure 2.3 we show the direction +30°, which is the same as the -330° direction, and the direction −120°, which is the same as the direction 240°. Here we chose to use values between −180° and 180° (between −π and π radians).

Whatever value you choose for a direction with angle θ, cosθ and sinθ will give the correct values of the projections of a unit vector in that direction along the x and y axes. The projection along the x axis, cosθ, will be positive if the direction is in the first or fourth quadrants (see figure 2.3), or negative if it is in the second or third quadrants. The projection along the y axis, sinθ, is positive in the first and second quadrants and negative in the third and fourth quadrants. Any unit vector C^ can then be written as,

C^=cosθı^+sinθȷ^
(2.2)

where θ is the angle that C^ does with the x axis. Vector C→, which is the product of its magnitude C times the unit vector C^, can be written as the sum of two vectors C→x and C→y, in the directions of ı^ and ȷ^:

C→=C→x+C→y=Cxı^+Cyȷ^
(2.3)

where C→x and C→y are called rectangular component vectors and the rectangular components of the vector are

Cx=Ccosθ   Cy=Csinθ
(2.4)

Conversely, given the rectangular components Cx and Cy of a vector, we can find its magnitude and direction through,

C=Cx2+Cy2   tanθ=CyCx
(2.5)

But since tanθ=tan(θ+π), there will be directions in the first and third quadrants with the same value of tanθ and the same for directions in the second and fourth quadrant. The value of θ can then be computed as:

θ={arctan(CyCx)+πx≥0,x≥0arctan(CyCx)+πx<0,y≥0arctan(CyCx)−πx<0,y<0
(2.6)

Computer programs usually include and extra function to compute inverse tangents given two parameters, the two rectangular components, returning a result in agreement with equation 2.6.

2.2.1 Scalar multiplication

Using the associative property of scalar multiplication, the product of scalar k times a vector A→=Axı^+Ayȷ^ is equal to,

kA→=kAxı^+kAyȷ^
(2.7)

Thus, each rectangular component is multiplied by the scalar.

2.2.2 Vector addition in rectangular components

In rectangular components, the sum of A→ and B→ is,

A→+B→=Axı^+Ayȷ^+Bxı^+Byȷ^

And using the commutative property of vector addition and the distributive law of scalar multiplication, we arrive at the result:

A→+B→=(Ax+Bx)ı^+(Ay+By)ȷ^
(2.8)

Namely, each rectangular component of the sum is equal to the sum of the corresponding components of vectors A→ and B→. Figure 2.4 shows the vectors (in red), their component vectors (in blue) and the sum the vectors (in red). The same result is obtained by adding the vectors or by adding their component vectors instead.

Example 2.1

A plane moves 580 km in direction 56° north of the east during its first hour of flight and 740 km in direction 28° south of the west during its second hour of flight. Find the total displacement of the plane during those two hours, giving its magnitude and direction.

Solution: The following diagram shows the plane's displacements A→ and B→ during the first and the second hour, using x axis in the east direction and y axis in the north direction.

The rectangular components of those vectors, in km, are then,

Ax=580cos56∘=324.33  Ay=580sin56∘=480.84
Bx=740cos(−152∘)=−653.38  By=740sin(−152∘)=−347.41

And the total displacement is the sum of the two vectors:

A→+B→=(324.33−653.38)ı^+(480.84−347.41)ȷ^ =−329.0ı^+133.4ȷ^

The magnitude of that total displacement is,

And the angle it makes with the east direction is,

Namely, the total displacement was 355 km in the direction 22° north of the west.

2.2.3 Dot product in rectangular components

To derive the expression of the dot product in rectangular components, we first notice that since both ı^ and ȷ^ have magnitude equal to 1 and they are perpendicular to each other, we then have,

ı^·ı^=1 ȷ^·ȷ^=1 ı^·ȷ^=0
(2.9)

We can then apply the distributive law for the dot product:

A→·B→=(Axı^+Ayȷ^)·(Bxı^+Byȷ^)
=AxBxı^·ı^+AxByı^·ȷ^+AyBxȷ^·ı^+AyByȷ^·ȷ^

Which leads us to the result,

A→·B→=AxBx+AyBy
(2.10)

2.2.4 Cross product in rectangular components

The cross product of any vector with itself is zero and the cross product of two perpendicular vectors has magnitude 1 and is perpendicular to those vectors. Therefore, we have the following properties for the rectangular unit vectors:

ı^×ı^=0→  ȷ^×ȷ^=0→  ı^×ȷ^=k^
(2.11)

Where k^ is the unit vector perpendicular to the plane xy, in the direction of the right-hand rule from ı^ to ȷ^ (see figure 2.5).

In rectangular coordinates, the cross product between vectors A→ and B→ is,

A→×B→=(Axı^+Ayȷ^)×(Bxı^+Byȷ^)
=AxBxı^×ı^+AxByı^×ȷ^+AyBxȷ^×ı^+AyByȷ^×ȷ^

And the result is:

A→×B→=(AxBy−AyBx)k^
(2.12)

Therefore, the magnitude of the cross product between the two vectors can be written as the absolute value of a determinant where the fist row are the rectangular components of A→ and the second row are the rectangular components of B→:

|A→×B→|=absolute value of |AxAyBxBy|
(2.13)

If the determinant above is positive, the cross product A→×B→ is in the direction k^ and if it is negative, the cross product is in the direction −k^.

Example 2.2

Find the area of the triangle PQR and the distance from point P to the side QR.

Solution: The two vectors PQ→ and PR→ on the sides of the triangle form a parallelogram whose area is twice the area of the triangle. Therefore, the area of the triangle is equal two one half of the magnitude of the cross product between PQ→ and PR→. The components of those vectors can be read directly from the figure, leading to the following result:

Area=12|PQ→×PR→|=12|3−0.543|=5.5

The distance from P to the side QR is the height of the triangle, when QR is considered its base. Thus, that distance is equal to twice the area of the triangle, divided by the length of side QR:

d=2×5.5QR→·QR→=2×5.512+3.52=3.02

2.3 Vectors in space

A vector A→ in space can be written as the sum of three component vectors A→x, A→y and A→z, in the directions of the unit vectors ı^, ȷ^ and k^ (figure 2.6).

Figure 2.6 shows the three rectangular component vectors; the three rectangular components Ax, Ay and Az are the projections of the vector along the x, y and z axis and we have,

Ax=Acosθx Ay=Acosθy Az=Acosθz
(2.14)

Where θx, θy and θz, called director cosines, are the angles that the vector does with each of the three axes. Notice that the director cosines are the rectangular components of the unit vector A^

A^=cosθxı^+cosθyȷ^+cosθzk^
(2.15)

and, since its magnitude is one,

cos2θx+cos2θy+cos2θz=1
(2.16)

Namely, we only need to know two of the angles θx, θy and θz in order to know the direction of A→. It is straight forward to generalize the expressions for the sum and products of vectors on a plane to the case of vectors in space:

pA→=pAxı^+pAyȷ^+pAzk^
(2.17)
A→+B→=(Ax+Bx)ı^ +(Ay+By)ȷ^ +(Az+Bz)k^
(2.18)
A→·B→=AxBx+AyBy+AzBz
(2.19)
A→×B→=|ı^ȷ^k^AxAyAzBxByBz|
(2.20)

2.4 Position

The position of a point P in space can also be given as three coordinates (x, y, z), which are the distances from the point to the three planes yz, xz and xy, as shown in figure 2.7. One can think of those three planes as two walls and the floor in a room.

There is a difference between the rectangular coordinates (x, y, z) of a point, and the rectangular components (Ax, Ay, Az) of a vector. The coordinates depend on the position of the origin (O in figure 2.7); a different choice of origin gives different coordinates for the same point. On the other hand, the rectangular components of a vector depend on the directions chosen for the three axes, but not on the position of the origin.

The position of a point with coordinates (x, y, z) can also be represented by a vector r→, called position vector, defined as:

r→=xı^+yȷ^+zk^
(2.21)

Figure 2.8 shows the position vector of point P, which is a vector from the origin O to point P. We have used a lower-case letter and a different color for the position vector, because it is not a real vector. For instance, the product of a scalar by a position vector or the sum of two position vectors have no physical meaning because they would depend on the choice of the origin.

However, the subtraction of two position vectors does have a physical meaning, which is the displacement vector from one point to another (figure 2.9), independent of the choice of the origin.

The displacement A→ from point P to point Q is equal to the position vector of Q minus the position vector o P:

A→=PQ→=r→Q−r→R
(2.22)

Which can also be written as r→Q=r→P+PQ→, showing that a position vector plus a displacement vector leads to another position vector. Other meaningful operation among position vectors is a weighted sum of position vectors, with the sum of all weights equal to one:

r→=∑i=1nkir→ik1+…+kn=1
(2.23)

Which is a valid position vector. If the constants ki are all positive, r→ is the position of the center of mass of a system of particles of masses mi, in the positions r→i, and ki is the mass mi divided by the total mass. Another important case is when the vectors r→i are on a plane and all constants ki are equal; in that case, equation (2.23) gives the position of the centroid of the polygon with vertices in r→i.
