2.1 Unit vectors

A unit vector is a vector with magnitude equal to 1. Thus, what characterizes different unit vectors is their direction and any possible direction in space can be characterized by a unit vector. If we divide a vector A→ by its magnitude, the result is a unit vector, in the direction of A→, which we will dubbed as A^, with a circumflex accent rather than an arrow, to distinguish it from A→:

A^=A→A
(2.1)

Vector A→ is then characterize with a positive scalar A, its magnitude, and a unit vector A^ which defines its direction (see figure 2.1). The vector is the product of its magnitude and its unit vector: A→=AA^.

2.2 Vectors on a plane

If we choose a particular direction on the plane and call the unit vector in that direction ı^, any other unit vectors A^, B^, etc. can be defined with just one number, the angle between those unit vectors and ı^. In figure 2.2, the unit vector A^ makes and angle α with the vector ı^, in the counter clock-wise direction. Rotating an angle α from the direction of ı^ in the clock-wise direction leads to another direction different from A^; to distinguish those two directions, we give a positive sign to one of them and a negative sign to the other. The unit vector ȷ^, perpendicular to ı^ defines the direction in which we consider the angle with ı^ positive. The angle β of unit vector B^ in figure 2.2 is then negative.

The directions of the two unit vectors ı^ and ȷ^ are usually called the x and y directions. Notice that every direction in space can be defined by a positive or a negative angle. In figure 2.3 we show the direction +30°, which is the same as the -330° direction, and the direction −120°, which is the same as the direction 240°. Here we chose to use values between −180° and 180° (between −π and π radians).

Whatever value you choose for a direction with angle θ, cosθ and sinθ will give the correct values of the projections of a unit vector in that direction along the x and y axes. The projection along the x axis, cosθ, will be positive if the direction is in the first or fourth quadrants (see figure 2.3), or negative if it is in the second or third quadrants. The projection along the y axis, sinθ, is be positive in the first and second quadrants and negative in the third and fourth quadrants. Any unit vector C^ can then be written as,

C^=cosθı^+sinθȷ^
(2.2)

where θ is the angle that C^ does with the x axis. Vector C→, which is the product of its module C times the unit vector C^ can be written as the sum of two vectors C→x and C→y, in the directions of ı^ and ȷ^:

C→=C→x+C→y=Cxı^+Cyȷ^
(2.3)

where C→x and C→y are called the rectangular component vectors of the vector and its rectangular components are

Cx=Ccosθ   Cy=Csinθ
(2.4)

Conversely, given the rectangular components Cx and Cy of a vector, we can find its magnitude and direction through,

C=Cx2+Cy2   tanθ=CyCx
(2.5)

But since tanθ=tan(θ+π), there will be directions in the first and third quadrants with the same value of tanθ and the same for directions in the second and fourth quadrant. The value of θ can then be computed as:

θ={arctan(CyCx)+πx≥0,x≥0arctan(CyCx)+πx<0,y≥0arctan(CyCx)−πx<0,y<0
(2.6)

Computer programs usually include and extra function to compute inverse tangents given two parameters, the two rectangular components, returning the result according to equation 2.6.

2.2.1 Scalar multiplication

Using the associative property of scalar multiplication, the product of scalar k by a vector A→=Axı^+Ayȷ^ is equal to,

kA→=kAxı^+kAyȷ^
(2.7)

Thus, each rectangular component is multiplied by the scalar.

2.2.2 Vector addition in rectangular components

In rectangular components, the sum of A→ and B→ is,

A→+B→=Axı^+Ayȷ^+Bxı^+Byȷ^

And using the commutative property of vector addition and the distributive law of scalar multiplication, we arrive at the result:

A→+B→=(Ax+Bx)ı^+(Ay+By)ȷ^
(2.8)

Namely, each rectangular component of the sum is equal to the sum of the corresponding components of vectors A→ and B→. Figure 2.4 shows the vectors (in red), their component vectors (in blue) and the sum the vectors (in red). The same result is obtained by adding the vectors or by adding their component vectors instead.

2.2.3 Dot product in rectangular components

To derive the expression of the dot product in rectangular components, we first notice that since both ı^ and ȷ^ have magnitude equal to 1 and they are perpendicular to each other, we then have,

ı^·ı^=1 ȷ^·ȷ^=1 ı^·ȷ^=0
(2.9)

We can then apply the distributive law for the dot product:

A→·B→=(Axı^+Ayȷ^)·(Bxı^+Byȷ^)
=AxBxı^·ı^+AxByı^·ȷ^+AyBxȷ^·ı^+AyByȷ^·ȷ^

Which leads us to the result,

A→·B→=AxBx+AyBy
(2.10)

2.2.4 Cross product in rectangular components

Since the cross product of any vector with itself is zero, in particular we have,

ı^×ı^=0→  ȷ^×ȷ^=0→  ı^×ȷ^=k^
(2.11)

Where k^ is the unit vector perpendicular to the plane xy, in the direction of the right-hand rule from ı^ to ȷ^ (see figure 2.5).

In rectangular coordinates, the cross product between vectors A→ and B→ is,

A→×B→=(Axı^+Ayȷ^)×(Bxı^+Byȷ^)
=AxBxı^×ı^+AxByı^×ȷ^+AyBxȷ^×ı^+AyByȷ^×ȷ^

And the result is:

A→×B→=(AxBy−AyBx)k^
(2.12)

Therefore, the magnitude of the cross product between the two vectors can be written as the absolute value of a determinant where the fist row are the rectangular components of A→ and the second row are the rectangular components of B→:

|A→×B→|=absolute value of |AxAyBxBy|
(2.13)

If the determinant above is positive, the cross product A→×B→ is in the direction k^ and if it is negative, the cross product is in the direction −k^.
